This is the negotiation interface from my behavioral experiments on human–AI negotiation (see the technical paper). You play the Supplier; the Retailer is an AI agent. Its decisions — when to accept and what to counter-offer — come from the experiment's rule-based engine; its chat messages are generated by a free LLM through a proxy (see Settings below). Chat is non-binding: only offers sent through the interface table, or confirmed there, are binding. No data is collected on this page.

The setting. You negotiate a wholesale contract: a wholesale price w (between 1–5€) and an order quantity q (between 1–100 units). The Production Cost is PC = 1.00€ per unit (common knowledge). Demand D is uniform on [0, 100]. As the Supplier you carry the inventory risk: you produce the full quantity q up front, but only the units that meet demand, min(q, D), are paid for.

Expected profits (as introduced in the experiment):
  Supplier:  πS = w · E[min(q, D)] − PC · q
  Retailer:  πR = (RP − w) · E[min(q, D)]
The Retail Price (RP) is the Retailer's private information: it can be 4€ or 5€, and the AI counterpart is not disclosing it. (For this demo: the AI has been assigned an RP of 4€ — it negotiates without revealing this.)

How the AI decides (transparency)

Shown here for transparency — participants in the actual experiment do not see this.

Benchmark. The AI negotiates toward the Nash bargaining solution computed from its (undisclosed) Retail Price of 4€ and the common-knowledge Production Cost of 1€: a wholesale price of 2.80€ and 75 units, worth an expected profit of 56.25 to each side. That profit is its target.

Acceptance rule. It accepts any offer — from chat or the interface — whose expected Retailer profit, (RP − w) · E[min(q, D)], is at least the target of 56.25. Accepted chat offers are re-posted in the interface as a binding offer for you to CONFIRM; accepted interface offers close the deal directly.

Counter-offer rules. If your offer falls short, the AI diagnoses why and counters — and every counter-offer it makes is placed in the interface as its binding proposal:

  • Price too high (quantity workable): it keeps your quantity and offers the highest wholesale price that still reaches its target profit.
  • Quantity unworkable at your price: it keeps your price and offers the quantity that reaches its target while maximizing your expected profit.
  • Both unworkable, or no readable offer: it proposes the Nash benchmark offer itself (2.80€, 75 units).

Partial offers. If your chat message names only a wholesale price, the AI completes it with the profit-maximizing quantity that meets its target; if it names only a quantity, the AI completes it with the corresponding price. Terms outside the allowed ranges (1–5€, 1–100 units) are rejected with a request to adjust.

Division of labor. These rules make every decision; the LLM only phrases the chat messages. Each generated reply is validated to contain the exact rule-computed terms before it is shown — otherwise a scripted message is used. The LLM cannot accept, reject, or change an offer.
